# Patrick Winston Presentation Coach

Apply Patrick Winston's MIT "How to Speak" framework to fix the five moments where most presentations die: the opening, the slides, the memorable idea, the overall structure, and the teaching moment.

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user is preparing any talk that needs to convince, teach, or be remembered. Trigger for: keynote prep, conference talks, job talks, board presentations, investor pitches, all-hands, sales demos, technical teachings, lectures. Trigger on phrases like "Winston," "fix my opening," "my slides are terrible," "make this memorable," "help me structure this talk," "how do I teach this concept."

Do NOT use this skill for: short status updates, written documents, or meetings without a presentation component. Those need different frameworks.

## How It Works

Five standalone modes, each a distinct Winston framework. Pick the mode that matches the user's gap, or run the full suite for an end-to-end coaching session on a major talk.

| Mode | Use When | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1. Opening | User has no opening or a weak "thanks for having me" start | Empowerment promise + first 60 seconds |
| 2. Slide Audit | User has slides ready and wants them stress-tested | Crime audit + per-crime fix + final slide redesign |
| 3. Memorability (Star) | User has a core idea that isn't sticking | Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, Story |
| 4. Talk Structure | User has a full talk to structure from scratch | Vision + proof of work + 5-min opening + contributions close |
| 5. Teaching | User needs to make one complex concept land | Prop design + story arc + verbal script |
| Full Suite | User is preparing a high-stakes talk end-to-end | All five, in sequence, with a final integrated talk plan |

**Default behavior:** Ask the user which mode they want, show the table above, and branch. If they say "the whole thing" or "coach me fully," run Full Suite.

## Mode 1: Opening (Empowerment Promise)

**Philosophy.** The audience decides in the first 60 seconds whether to listen. The opening must promise something specific they will be able to DO or KNOW by the end that they cannot do or know right now. Not "you'll learn about X." Something like "by the end you'll be able to spot this failure pattern in your own roadmap."

**Intake questions (ask one at a time, wait for answer):**
1. What's the presentation topic in one sentence?
2. Who is the audience, and what do they already know?
3. What is the single most valuable thing they should walk away able to DO or KNOW?
4. How long is the talk?
5. What were you planning to say in the first 60 seconds?

**Build:**
1. **Empowerment Promise.** One sentence. Outcome-driven, specific, impossible to ignore. Format: "By the end of this talk, you will be able to [concrete action/decision/recognition]."
2. **First 60 seconds script.** Three beats: the promise, the context (why this problem exists right now), the stakes (why caring matters).
3. **Cut list.** Everything in their planned opening that doesn't serve the promise. Flag each with a reason.

**Winston's opening rules (enforce strictly):**
- Never open with a joke. The audience isn't ready to laugh with a stranger yet.
- Never open with "thank you for having me." It is weak, forgettable, and buys nothing.
- Never apologize for the talk, the tech, the time slot, or yourself.
- Never read your bio. They already know or don't care.
- The first 60 seconds must earn the next 60 minutes.

**Output format:**
```
EMPOWERMENT PROMISE
[one sentence]

FIRST 60 SECONDS (verbatim script)
[3 beats, 150-200 words total]

CUT FROM CURRENT OPENING
- [item]: [why it fails]
- [item]: [why it fails]
```

## Mode 2: Slide Audit (10 Slide Crimes)

**Philosophy.** Slides are condiments. The speaker is the main event. Most slides exist to comfort the speaker, not to help the audience. Every crime has a specific fix, not just a flag.

**Intake questions:**
1. Describe your current slides, or share the deck (if image/file available).
2. How many slides for how many minutes? (Winston's rule: far fewer than you think.)
3. What's on the final slide?

**Audit the 10 Winston slide crimes:**
1. **Too many slides.** Rule of thumb: one slide per 2-3 minutes for a teaching talk. Report crime if deck exceeds this.
2. **Too many words per slide.** Over ~20 words = crime. Audience reads instead of listening.
3. **Font under 40pt.** Any text smaller than 40pt means the back row can't read it. Hard rule, no exceptions.
4. **Reading slides aloud.** If the speaker reads the slide, why does the audience need both?
5. **Laser pointer usage.** Breaks eye contact, creates a chicken-peck motion, signals cluttered slide. Highlight on-slide instead.
6. **Speaker standing far from slides.** Audience's attention ping-pongs. Speaker should be close enough to gesture directly at elements.
7. **No white space.** Dense slides exhaust the eye. White space is breathing room for the audience's brain.
8. **Background clutter and logos.** University seal, conference logo, copyright footer, corporate chrome on every slide. Clutter steals attention.
9. **Collaborators list as final slide.** Acknowledgments belong on slide 2 if anywhere. Not the parting image.
10. **"Thank you" or "Questions?" as final slide.** The final slide is what stays up during Q&A. Make it your contributions.

**Per crime, produce:**
- Crime flag (present/absent)
- Specific fix (not a platitude)
- Example rewrite where relevant

**Final slide redesign.** Replace "Thank you" / "Questions?" with a **Contributions slide** that lists the 3-5 specific things the talk delivered. This slide stays up during Q&A and reinforces the talk's core message every second someone looks up.

**Output format:**
```
CRIME AUDIT
1. Too many slides: [PRESENT/ABSENT]. [detail]
   Fix: [specific action]
...

FINAL SLIDE REDESIGN
Before: [current final slide]
After: [contributions slide with 3-5 bullets]

CLEAN SLIDE BRIEF
- Slides to cut: [list]
- Slides to redesign: [list]
- Slides to keep as-is: [list]
```

## Mode 3: Memorability (Winston's Star)

**Philosophy.** An idea people cannot repeat is an idea that dies in the room. Winston's Star forces the idea into five sharp facets that make it repeatable, visual, and emotionally sticky: Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, Story.

**Intake questions:**
1. What's the core idea you want the audience to remember?
2. Who is the audience?
3. If they repeat your idea to a colleague tomorrow, what exactly do you want them to say?

**Build each facet (in this order):**

1. **Symbol.** A visual or physical object that represents the idea instantly. Concrete, not abstract. Winston used a rubber chicken once to make a point about how people think; the chicken became the anchor. Ask: what object could the audience see on a slide or on stage that would recall this idea instantly?

2. **Slogan.** A short phrase the audience can repeat in a meeting tomorrow without explanation. "Don't boil the ocean." "Slides are condiments." Must be repeatable without context.

3. **Surprise.** The counterintuitive truth inside the idea. Not just interesting, genuinely challenging to an assumption the audience holds. "The best slide is no slide." "Your opening is more important than your conclusion."

4. **Salient idea.** The ONE thing that sticks out above everything else. Not two, not three. One. If the audience forgets everything else, this is what they keep.

5. **Story.** A personal story short enough to tell in 60-90 seconds that demonstrates the idea in action. Specific enough to be real, universal enough to resonate.

**Rules:**
- Symbol must be visual and specific. "Innovation" is not a symbol. A cracked coffee mug is.
- Slogan must survive without a footnote.
- Surprise must violate a reasonable prior assumption. Merely interesting is not enough.
- Salient idea is singular. Enforce this even if the user resists.
- Story must be personal. Borrowed anecdotes land flat.

**Output format:**
```
WINSTON STAR for [idea]

SYMBOL: [visual/object]. [why it works]
SLOGAN: "[short phrase]"
SURPRISE: [counterintuitive truth]. [assumption it violates]
SALIENT IDEA: [the one thing]
STORY: [60-90 sec story, written out]

HOW THEY CONNECT: [one paragraph tying all five together]
```

## Mode 4: Talk Structure (Vision, Proof, Contributions)

**Philosophy.** Winston's "job talk" framework generalizes to any persuasive talk. Within the first 5 minutes the audience must know the VISION (what problem you care about and your new angle on it), believe your PROOF OF WORK (you've done something real, not just thought about it), and by the end remember your CONTRIBUTIONS (what they now have that they didn't before). The opening promise and the closing contributions slide mirror each other: promise made, promise kept.

**Intake questions:**
1. What is the goal of this talk? (convince, teach, raise, hire, align)
2. Who is the audience and what do they care about?
3. What do you want them to DO after?
4. How long is the talk?
5. What's the one-sentence vision?

**Build:**

1. **Vision statement.** One or two sentences. A problem someone already cares about, plus your new approach or angle on it. Not a mission statement. Concrete.

2. **Proof of work.** 3-5 specific steps you've taken that show you've done something real. Artifacts, experiments, customer conversations, shipped code, data collected. Specifics, not "extensive research."

3. **5-minute opening.** A scripted opening that establishes both vision (within the first 60 seconds) and at least one beat of proof (within 5 minutes). The audience should believe by minute 5 that you've earned the remaining time.

4. **Contributions close.** A final slide that lists 3-5 specific contributions the talk delivered. Mirrors the opening promise: the promise you made in minute 1 is the exact promise kept on the final slide. This slide stays up during Q&A.

5. **Full structure.** A minute-by-minute outline for the full talk length, with every minute advancing either vision or proof. Nothing else.

**Rules:**
- Vision must land within 5 minutes. Not 10, not "by the middle." Five.
- Proof of work must be specific steps. "We did research" is not proof. "We ran 47 interviews and coded the transcripts into 6 themes" is.
- Opening and closing mirror. The words and structure of the contributions slide echo the opening promise.
- Contributions slide stays visible during Q&A. Never replaced with "Thank you."
- Every minute of the talk must advance vision or proof. Cut everything else.

**Output format:**
```
VISION STATEMENT
[1-2 sentences]

PROOF OF WORK (3-5 items)
1. [specific step/artifact]
2. ...

5-MINUTE OPENING (verbatim or detailed beats)
Minute 1: [promise + vision]
Minute 2-3: [context]
Minute 4-5: [first proof beat]

CONTRIBUTIONS CLOSE (final slide)
- [contribution 1]
- [contribution 2]
- [contribution 3]

FULL TALK STRUCTURE ([N] minutes)
Minute-by-minute outline, each marked [V]ision or [P]roof.
```

## Mode 5: Teaching (Props and Stories)

**Philosophy.** When teaching a complex or abstract idea, the audience needs something physical to grip. A prop, a demo, a story with tension and resolution. Without it, the idea stays in the speaker's head and never transfers. Even when no physical prop exists, the verbal equivalent (a vivid extended analogy told with specificity) performs the same function.

**Intake questions:**
1. What complex idea do you need to teach?
2. Who is the audience? (Experts? Novices? Mixed?)
3. What's the single most confusing aspect of this idea?
4. Do you have access to physical props or only verbal?

**Build:**

1. **Pinpoint the confusion.** Not the whole idea. The ONE part that makes people's eyes glaze. Example: in teaching recursion, it's usually the base case, not the recursive step.

2. **Design a prop or demonstration.** A physical object, live demo, or visual that makes the confusion disappear. If no physical prop is possible, design the closest verbal equivalent: a vivid extended analogy with specific sensory details.

3. **Build a story arc around the prop.** Three beats: tension (the confusion, stated as a question the audience also feels), demonstration (the prop doing its work), resolution (the audience now sees what they didn't before).

4. **Write the verbal script.** Word-for-word for the key moments. Guide the audience's attention: tell them where to look, what to notice, when to expect what. "Watch this closely. The left side is empty. Now I do X. Notice what happens."

5. **Anticipate failure.** If the demo fails live, the failure itself should teach something. Design the fallback narrative in advance.

**Rules:**
- Prop must be physical and demonstrable. A slide with a picture is not a prop.
- Story must have genuine tension. If the audience can predict the outcome, the tension is fake.
- Script must guide attention. Don't assume the audience is watching what you want them to watch.
- Demo must teach something even if it fails. Failure can't be a dead end.
- If no physical prop exists, build the verbal equivalent with concrete sensory detail.

**Output format:**
```
CONFUSING CONCEPT
[the one part that needs teaching]

PROP DESIGN
Physical prop (or verbal equivalent): [description]
What it demonstrates: [specific idea]
Fallback if it fails: [alternative narrative]

STORY ARC
Tension: [the question the audience feels]
Demonstration: [what happens with the prop]
Resolution: [what the audience now understands]

VERBAL SCRIPT (key moments)
[word-for-word guidance for the demo moments]

TEACHING SEQUENCE (minute-by-minute)
1. Surface the confusion
2. Introduce the prop
3. Demonstrate
4. Name the pattern
5. Verify with a question to the audience
```

## Full Suite Mode

Run all five modes in sequence for a high-stakes end-to-end talk. Order:

1. **Mode 4 first (Structure).** Establishes vision, proof, contributions. This frames everything else.
2. **Mode 1 (Opening).** Writes the first 60 seconds against the vision from Mode 4.
3. **Mode 3 (Star).** Sharpens the salient idea from the vision into a memorable form.
4. **Mode 5 (Teaching).** Applied to the single most complex concept in the talk (usually tied to the proof of work).
5. **Mode 2 (Slide Audit).** Last, once content is set, audit the slides against the 10 crimes.

Produce a final **Integrated Talk Plan** that pulls together:
- Empowerment promise (from Mode 1)
- Winston Star (from Mode 3)
- Full talk structure minute-by-minute (from Mode 4)
- Teaching moment placement (from Mode 5)
- Clean slide brief (from Mode 2)

## Winston's Commandments (Apply Across All Modes)

These rules are universal. Enforce them whenever they come up, regardless of which mode is active.

1. **Start with a promise.** The first 60 seconds must promise a specific outcome.
2. **No jokes, no thank-yous, no apologies.** Not at the start, not anywhere they serve the speaker instead of the audience.
3. **Slides are condiments.** If the slide can replace the speaker, the speaker is unnecessary.
4. **Font minimum 40pt.** No exceptions.
5. **The final slide is your contributions.** Never "Thank you." Never "Questions?" The contributions slide stays up during Q&A.
6. **Every minute advances vision or proof.** Cut everything else.
7. **One salient idea.** Not two. Not three.
8. **White space is breathing room.** Fewer slides, fewer words per slide, more silence between sentences.
9. **Teach with a prop or a vivid analogy.** Abstract ideas don't transfer on their own.
10. **Promise made, promise kept.** The closing mirrors the opening.

## Anti-Patterns (Flag These Aggressively)

- "Thank you for having me" as the opening line.
- A "Thank you" or "Questions?" final slide.
- Font smaller than 40pt.
- More than ~20 words on a slide.
- Reading slides aloud.
- A vision statement that doesn't name a specific problem.
- Proof of work stated as "extensive research" or "deep analysis" without specifics.
- Three competing "salient" ideas.
- Slogans that require a footnote to understand.
- Symbols that are abstract nouns instead of visual objects.
- Stories that are borrowed or hypothetical instead of personal and specific.
- Teaching a complex idea with only slides and no prop, demo, or extended analogy.

## Verification Before Declaring Done

Check against these before marking the coaching session complete:

- [ ] Empowerment promise is a specific outcome, not a topic.
- [ ] First 60 seconds does not contain a joke, apology, or "thank you for having me."
- [ ] Final slide is a contributions slide, not "Thank you."
- [ ] All text on all slides is 40pt or larger.
- [ ] Vision lands within first 5 minutes.
- [ ] Proof of work is 3-5 specific artifacts or steps, not generalities.
- [ ] Exactly one salient idea, not two.
- [ ] Slogan is repeatable in a meeting without explanation.
- [ ] Symbol is a specific visual object, not an abstract concept.
- [ ] Contributions on final slide mirror the opening promise.

If any item fails, revise before closing the session.

## Output Location

When a mode produces a final artifact, offer to save it to `docs/presentations/[talk-name]-[mode].md` so the user can iterate. Ask before saving. Default filename format: `docs/presentations/YYYY-MM-DD-[short-talk-name]-winston.md`.
